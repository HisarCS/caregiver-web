Our Features

Caregiver evaluates the user using 5 types of questions. Detection of user input is made by the combination of two different models.

Text-Input Based

Text-input based questions are evualted without checking grammer or spelling

Game Based

Gamification encourages active participation and better comprehension. The mobile environment enhances controlled administration of the test.

Visual-Input Based

Visual-input based questions are evaluated using the weighted combination of distinct models. These questions include drawing an analog clock and an abstract shape.

Orientation and Memory Based

Orientation and memory based questions include telling the date and remembering phrases throughout the test.

Abstract

Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is an invasive neurodegenerative disorder that has no cure. The treatment of AD is based on drugs, which have to be administered in the early stages of the development of the disease. Therefore, early diagnosis in the mild cognitive impairment (MCI) stage of the disease is of great significance for the efficiency of the treatment. AD is currently diagnosed by the provision of a professional, conducting medical tests, which is not easily accessible. In this paper, we introduce Caregiver, an application that takes initiative by facilitating the administration of the Self-Administered Gerocognitive Exam (SAGE), a test for detecting AD designed and used by the Wexner Medical Centre, on mobile devices through a user-friendly digital environment. Its user interface enables active interaction through gamification where 5 different types of questions are asked to the user to evaluate their orientation, language, reasoning, executive function, and memory skills. Points are assigned to each question using a distinct algorithm. Detection of visual inputs, such as drawing abstract shapes and analog clocks, are done using a weighted combination of two different classification models, hence enabling the user to conduct the test without the provision of a medical professional. Therefore, patients are able to self-administer and get diagnosed in a matter of seconds. The current prototype suggests its users to consult medical professionals if MCI is detected. By carrying the test in a mobile device, Caregiver both increases the accessibility of AD diagnosis by providing essential healthcare to people that don’t have access to medical faculties and the accuracy of the test by conducting it in a controlled environment detached from human supervision.
